    Pest Name: Corn Borer

    Information

    The Corn borer spends the winter as a fully formed larva in corn stalks and other plant waste, such as weed stems. The mature larva measures approximately 1 inch (25 mm) in length, has a creamy to grey hue, and is distinguished by a few sparse rows of tiny, round, brown dots that run the size of its body.


    Symptoms:

    1. All of the plant's aerial parts are damaged by the larvae. They tunnel into the stalks at the base of the plant to eat first, then feed later on silk and ear tissues. The result is reduced development, fewer leaves, and decreased output because they obliterate interior tissues transporting water and nutrients.

    2. The plant's ability to support itself is weakened by the stalk's tunneling, which encourages lodging. The ears have circular holes with damaged, damp frass and maybe dropping kernels.

    3. Opportunistic fungi use the perforations to colonize the plant, leading to rot in those tissues. The toxins these fungi create further degrade the yield's quality.



    Organic Control:

    It is feasible to manage populations of the European Corn Borer with predators, parasitoids, and bio-insecticides. The local predators are insidious flower bugs (Orius insidiosus), green lacewings, and several ladybirds. Additionally, 20 to 30 percent of the overwintering larvae might be wiped out by birds. Additionally, effective are bio-insecticides based on Bacillus thuringiensis or Spinosad.
