    Pest Name: Pink Boll-worm

    Information

    The pink boll-worm is an insect known for being a pest in cotton farming. The moth's wings are fringed, a little, thin grey adult. The larva is an eight-legged, dull-white caterpillar with prominent pink banding around its dorsum. The larva grows to a length of half an inch.


    Symptoms:

    1. The pink bollworm prevents buds from opening, sheds bolls, damages lint, and destroys seeds. The initial generation of larvae feeds in squares in the early summer, where they continue to grow and create blooms.

    2. The petals of the infected blossoms may be bound together by threads made of larval silk. To consume the seeds, the second generation of larvae pierces the lint and enters the bolls.

    3. Warts inside the carpal walls are another sign of damage on bolls. Additionally, unlike a bollworm, which frequently hollows out the boll and leaves frass on the outside, the larvae do not accomplish this.



    Organic Control:

    Infested crops can be sprayed with sex pheromones made from Pectinophora gossypiella. This makes it far more difficult for male worms to find females and mate. Spinosad or Bacillus thuringiensis spraying applied when necessary can also be efficient. From 45 days after sowing or at the flowering stage, pheromone traps (8 per acre) can be set. They can be left in place until the last picking or the end of the crop period. Every 21 days, the lures on the traps should be changed.
