    Pest Name: Sugarcane Internode Borer

    Information

    The Sugarcane Internode Borer, also known as Chilo sacchari-phagus, is a significant pest of sugarcane, maize, and sorghum. By boring into the stems of these hosts' plants, its caterpillars cause harm. Moths known as sugarcane internode borer have 24-36 mm wingspans and tiny, yellow-brown bodies. This species's drab, light-brown forewings show one or two dark dots. Male moths have dirty-white to light-brown hindwings, whereas female moths have silky-white hindwings.


    Symptoms:

    1. The caterpillars initially eat the immature, rolled leaves, which result in shot holes. They consume the growing points that produce the dead hearts in the early stages of plant development.

    2. Several boreholes are used to shorten and restrict internodes. They obstruct the access openings with excrement when they enter the stems and feed inside.

    3. The larva climbs through the stem tissues, producing inflammation and node damage. Plant stalks are vulnerable to wind damage and are readily damaged. Another indication is a decrease in growth.



    Organic Control:

    There are currently no known biological insecticides for this pest, but parasitoids can lower internode borer occurrence. Release Trichogramma cheilosis, an egg parasite, six times starting in the fourth month at intervals of 15 days. Stenobracon deesa and Apanteles flavipes are larval parasitoids. The parasitoids Tetrastichus ay yari and Trichospilus diatraeae can be released for the pupal stage.
