    Pest Name: Flea Beetle

    Information

    Flea beetles have huge rear legs that jump like fleas when attacked. They are tiny, shiny-coated beetles. Flea beetles come in a variety of species. While some species attack various plants, others exclusively strike a few families of plants. Many vegetable plants are vulnerable to these pests in the garden. Still, those in the Brassica family, like broccoli, cabbage, kale, radishes, and turnips, and Nightshades, like tomatoes, peppers, and eggplants, are particularly vulnerable.


    Symptoms:

    1. Adults eat leaves for food. Small, dispersed shot-like holes (1-2 mm) and small chewing cavities that do not cut through the leaf blade are the visible signs of damage (pitting).

    2. Around the injured tissue, there may be a faint discoloration.

    3. Depending on the species, tubers are bored with shallow, straight holes at varying depths. The surface of the tuber may also develop a few little raised bumps due to the injury.



    Organic Control:

    One can utilize fungus-based diseases, insecticidal soaps, or the bacterial pesticide Spinosad to control populations. Adult damsel bugs (Nabis spp.), lacewing (Chrysopa spp.) larvae, and several parasitoid wasps consume or kill adult flea beetles. Some nematodes also consume the soil-dwelling larvae.
