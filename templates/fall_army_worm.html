    Pest Name: Fall Army-worm

    Information

    The fall army-worm is a species of the Lepidoptera order and one of the fall army-worm moth species that their larval stage may identify. The word "army-worm" can refer to a variety of species and frequently describes the species' larval stage's widespread invasive nature.


    Symptoms:

    1. The fall armyworm's larvae eat all plant sections, which results in harm. Larvae that are still young. They initially only consume one side of the leaf tissue's surface, leaving the other side unharmed (window feeding).

    2. Armyworms eat up to the time when buds and growth points are destroyed, and seedlings can be fed. Larger larvae produce lines of larval frass, rows of perforations, and ragged margins on leaves that are distinctive to seedlings.

    3. Armyworms may also attack the fruit that is still developing or the plant's base. The larvae of the autumn armyworm can result in significant leaf loss in cases of excessive infestation.



    Organic Control:

    Cotesia marginiventris, Chelonus texanus, and C. remus are examples of wasp parasitoids. Archytas marmoratus is the parasitoid fl y that is most prevalent. Ground beetles, spined soldierbugs, flower bugs, birds, and rodents are some examples of predators. Spraying is an option for bio-insecticides that include neem extracts, Bacillus thuringiensis or Baculovirus Spodoptera, Spinosad, or Azadirachtin. When added to the whorl of corn, red sand, rock salt, charcoal powder, or fly ash prevent the larvae from feeding and ultimately kill them (100 percent, 98 percent, 90 and 80 percent efficacy, respectively).
