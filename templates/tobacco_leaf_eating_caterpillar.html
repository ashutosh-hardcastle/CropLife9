    Pest Name: Tobacco Leaf-eating Caterpillar

    Information

    The tobacco caterpillar is a polyphagous insect pest. It is a significant pest of the following plants: cowpea, potato, tomato, cabbage, and cauliflower. The larvae directly harm crops. After consuming the leaf's green tissue, they become grey, dark grey, or black. The tobacco leaf sustains 10 to 15 percent damage due to the adult larva's voracious appetite and extensive hole-making.


    Symptoms:

    1. On the leaves, feeding damage becomes evident. Oversized, asymmetrical holes are being made in the leaf tissue by larvae scratching it.

    2. The plant may completely lose all of its leaves as a result. Additionally attacked, flower buds and pods have eating holes.

    3. The roots also suffer harm in lighter soils. Only petioles and branches are left after intense feeding.

    Organic Control:

    Eggs and larvae can be treated using bioinsecticides based on Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus (NPV) or Bacillus thuringiensis. In the evening, rice bran, molasses, or brown sugar-based bait solutions can be sprayed on the ground. Neem leaves or kernel extracts, as well as extracts from Pongamia glabra seeds, are mighty against Spodoptera litura larvae. For instance, NSKE 5 per cent or azadirachtin 1500 ppm (5 ml/l) can be applied during the egg stage to stop the egg from hatching.
