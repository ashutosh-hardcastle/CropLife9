    Pest Name: Sugarcane Mealy Bug

    Information

    Mealybugs are oval, wingless insects found in warm or temperate climates. A thin layer of mealy wax that gives them a cottony look covers their body to protect it. They penetrate plant tissues with their long, sucking stylets and extract the sap from them. The poisonous compounds that they inject into plants when feeding cause the symptoms. The soil is where the mealy bug eggs are also placed. Nymphs and adults can crawl to nearby plants after hatching.


    Symptoms:

    1. The underside of leaves, stems, flowers, and fruits develop white cotton-like masses comprised of bug flocks. Young leaves can become yellow and curl, plants grow slowly, and fruits fall off early due to infestations.

    2. During sap-sucking, the bugs excrete honeydew, which makes the tissues sticky and more likely to be colonized by opportunistic bacteria and fungi.

    3. Fruits are especially vulnerable to attack and may develop defects or get fully covered in wax secretions. The honeydew may draw ants, transmitting the pest to other plants. Older leaves are less likely to wilt or change shape.

    Organic Control:

    Apply a cotton bud dipped in oil or spirit to the colonies of mealybugs at the first indication of a minor infestation. You can use insecticidal soap to spray the plants. Neem oil or pyrethrins should be sprayed on nearby plants to prevent the population from spreading. Green lacewings, parasitoid wasps, hoverflies, ladybird beetles, mealybug destroyers, and the predator butterfly Spalgis epius are natural enemies.
